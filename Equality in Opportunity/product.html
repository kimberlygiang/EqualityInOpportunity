SAT/ACT

Why you should take the SAT/ACT

Although the SAT and the ACT are optional tests, it is very important that you take them.
All colleges use your SAT/ACT scores for admissions - they use them to judge whether you'd be a good fit for
their school or not. It's almost better to have a bad score than to not take the test at all.
It is also possible to take the tests multiple times to obtain the best score possible.
Obviously, the better score you have,
the better chance you have of getting accepted into a better school.
This is why it's important that you take the tests and do the best as possible.

SAT

The SAT has a different makeup than the ACT. It contains more math and reading:

Math: 58 questions in 80 mins.
Reading: 52 questions in 65 mins.
Writing and Language: 44 questions in 35 mins.
Essay (optional): 1 essay 50 minutes
.

Scoring
The SAT has two big sections – Evidence-Based Reading and Writing, and Math. You can earn a scaled score of between 200 and 800 points on each section, for a total of 1600 possible points on the Redesigned SAT.

The scaled score of between 200 and 800 is converted from the raw score you earn on each section. Your raw score is simply the number of questions you answered correctly. Skipped or wrong questions do not add or subtract from your raw score.

The scaled score is out of 1600.

ACT

The ACT has a different makeup than the SAT. It contains more sections and different subjects.

Math: 60 questions in 60 mins.
Reading: 40 questions in 35 mins.
English: 75 questions in 45 mins.
Science: 40 questions in 35 mins.
Essay (optional): 1 essay in 40 mins.

Scoring
First the number of correctly answered questions are counted. Points are not deducted for incorrect answers. (There is no penalty for guessing.) This is your raw score.

The raw scores are converted to 'scale scores'. Scale Scores are scaled to be out of 36 points. They will be used to make up the Composite Score, which will be your final score.

The Composite score is the average of your four test scores, rounded to the nearest whole number out of 36. Fractions less than one-half are rounded down; fractions one-half or more are rounded up.
